Introduction

Welcome to Epiphany, a new blogging experience allowing the mix of text content with runnable programs.

The idea of Epiphany came after a series of inspirations, the two most obvious ones are Medium and Jupyter Notebook. I came across Jupyter Notebook through the NLP course by Richard Socher. I really like its concept of interleaving text with programs. I always think programs are superior in terms of understandability than bare math equations. After all, you can debug, step through and substitute inputs with a program, the experience is very interactive. Having a runnable program is like giving a kid a clock to dissemble and reassemble it back to figure out how the clock works. Whereas providing just the equations is like providing only the blueprints of that clock. I seriously believe equations and math symbols were optimized for write and derive on paper, it's not an user friendly form for reading and understanding. For example, math equations tend to use single character symbols followed by a paragraph to explain what those symbols mean and often the authors are too lazy to cover each of them. The likeness of Greek and English characters, the subtle differences in typography, such as bold vs. italic make reading academic papers a headache.

The author of this Atlantic article held the same view and asked the question: What would you get if you designed the scientific paper from scratch today? And I can't agree with the following more:

It was a shame that in mathematics it’s been a tradition for hundreds of years to make papers as formal and austere as possible, often suppressing the very visual aids that mathematicians use to make their discoveries.

While the idea of Epiphany was conceived, I also had inspirations from countless interactive blog posts. Just to mention a few of them to show you how powerful interactive contents are for better understanding:

On the technical side, Jupyter Notebook chose to use a backend server to run programs and return results, whereas Epiphany relies on pure web technology, because web today is powerful enough to carry out 90% what offline compute can do, especially with WebAssembly. Putting everything in the browser creates a more integrated experience and let us to take the advantage of the hundreds and thousands of javascript packages available already. Two of the recent projects, observable and iodide also use web technologies and had influenced my choice.

Finally, as a reader, I found myself read less books these days, but more from bloggers on sites like Medium. I found online content less pretentious, online writers are often humble and they share what they have learned and distill obscure concepts as my peers rather than from the above. I hope Epiphany could grow into a community for thinkers (as opposite to instagram). The codex platform Da Vinci would choose if he were a modern man.
